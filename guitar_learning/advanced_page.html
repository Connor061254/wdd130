Welcome to the Advanced Page

This is for the comfortable player who is ready for mastery. You are playing songs and kind of understand why it works, but you are ready to ask the big questions. This section is less about "what to play" and more about "finding your own voice." We will unlock the entire fretboard, dive into complex harmony, and refine your technique to a professional level.

Unlocking the Fretboard: The CAGED System

The CAGED system is the secret to finally being able to use the whole fretboard. Many players get stuck in "box patterns." CAGED shows you that the entire neck is just five simple open chords (C, A, G, E, D) connected together like puzzle pieces.

Watch this to see how the shapes connect up the neck.

How It Works

These aren't just chords. They are movable shapes. If you take an open C Major chord and move it up the neck using a barre, it becomes a D chord, then an E chord, but it keeps the "C Shape."

  • C Shape: Good for melodic fills
  • A Shape: The standard "Barre Chord" you likely already know (Root on A string).
  • G Shape: A wide stretch, great for arpeggios.
  • E Shape: The standard "Barre Chord" (Root on E string).
  • D Shape: Great for higher-register voicing and triads.

The Anatomy of a Chord

Before we dive deeper into modes, we need to stop looking at chords as "grips" and start seeing them as recipes. But to bake the cake (the chord), you first need the ingredients (the scale).

Step 1: The Major Scale Recipe (2-2-1)

You don't need to memorize every scale. You just need to memorize the spacing between the notes. On a guitar, a "2" means moving up 2 frets (a Whole Step), and a "1" means moving up 1 fret (a Half Step).

The Magic Number: 2 - 2 - 1 - 2 - 2 - 2 - 1

Start on any note on the Low E string. If you follow that pattern of frets, you will play the Major Scale for that key.

  • Start on G (3rd Fret):
  • Move up 2 frets -> A
  • Move up 2 frets -> B
  • Move up 1 fret -> C
  • Move up 2 frets -> D
  • Move up 2 frets -> E
  • Move up 2 frets -> F#
  • Move up 1 fret -> Back to G!

Step 2: The Triad (1-3-5)

Now that we have our scale (G - A - B - C - D - E - F#), we build the chord. A Major chord is always made of the 1st, 3rd, and 5th notes of that scale.

  • 1 (Root): G
  • 3 (The Color): B
  • 5 (The Support): D

That's it. Whether you play a massive barre chord or a tiny open chord, you are only playing these three notes, just repeated in different octaves.

Step 3: Voicing (Why the Root Rules)

You have 6 strings, but only 3 notes to play. This means we have to double up. But be carefulâ€”not all notes are created equal.

  • The Root (1) is the foundation: In a standard open G chord, you are actually playing the note G three times. This gives the chord power and stability.
  • The Third (3) is the "Spice": The 3rd note (B) is very strong. It determines if the chord is happy (Major) or sad (Minor). Because it is so colorful, using it too many times can make the chord sound muddy or weird. Usually, one "3rd" in a chord is enough!

Why Scales Matter

We just used a scale to build a chord. But scales are much more than just "ingredients lists." They are the DNA of music. A scale is simply a specific selection of notes that creates a mood for your song.

Why is the scale the way it is?

Why do we use the "2-2-1" pattern? It comes down to physics and gravity. Music is about Tension and Release.
The spacing of the notes in the Major scale creates a pull. When you play the 7th note of the scale, your ear physically needs to hear the 8th note (the Octave) to feel resolved. If scales were just random notes, music would have no emotional direction.

How to Understand Everything

Once you know the scale of a song (the "Key"), you have the map to the entire city:

  • Solos: You can play any note from that scale over the song, and it will sound "correct." No more hitting bad notes.
  • Chords: Just like we built the "1-3-5" chord, you can build chords off every single note in the scale. This tells you exactly which chords belong in the key and which ones don't.
  • Harmonies: Want to sing a harmony? Just pick the 3rd note above the melody note within the scale.

Where to Start: The "Parent" Scale

There are hundreds of scales, and it is easy to get overwhelmed. Ignore them for now.

You only need to master one: The Major Scale.

Why? Because almost every other scale is just a modification of the Major Scale:

  • Want the Minor Scale? It's just the Major Scale starting on the 6th note.
  • Want the Pentatonic Scale? It's just the Major Scale with two notes removed.
  • Want Modes? They are just the Major scale starting on different steps.

Master the Major scale, and you inadvertently master them all.

Modes: Flavors, Not Just Scales

Time to leave the Pentatonic box. Modes are "flavors" of the Major scale. By changing which note you emphasize, you change the entire mood.

Essential Modes

  • Dorian (The "Santana" Sound): It's a minor scale with a raised 6th note. It sounds cool, smooth, and jazzy.
  • Lydian (The "Dreamy" Sound): A major scale with a raised 4th. It sounds floaty, sci-fi, and magical (think Steve Vai or film scores).
  • Mixolydian (The "Rock" Sound): A major scale with a flat 7th. It sounds bluesy and tough (think AC/DC or Grateful Dead).

Harmony: 7ths, 9ths, & 11ths

If basic chords are "Primary Colors," extended chords are the high-definition details. This is how you get that pro R&B, Neo-Soul, or Jazz sound.

The Extensions

  • 7ths (The Jazz Standard): Adding the 7th note makes a chord sound "sophisticated" and smooth. Major 7ths are pretty; Dominant 7ths are bluesy.
  • 9ths (The "Sparkle"): Adding the 9th note adds a layer of emotion and "shimmer" to the chord.
  • 11ths & 13ths: These create deep, complex tension often used in funk and modern R&B.

Specialized Techniques: Choose Your Adventure

This is where you decide what kind of guitarist you want to be. You don't need to learn all of these, but mastering one will take you from "Good" to "Great."

For the Shredder

Sweep Picking: A technique where you "rake" the pick across strings in a single smooth motion while fretting arpeggios. Essential for Metal and Fusion.

Economy Picking: Combining alternate picking with sweeping to move across strings with zero wasted energy.

For the Acoustic Pro

Percussive Fingerstyle: Using the body of the guitar as a drum kit while you play. (Think Sungha Jung or Marcin).

Hybrid Picking: Using a pick (for bass notes) and your fingers (for melody) at the same time. Common in Country and modern Math Rock.

Don't forget about Slide Guitar, a completely different way to approach the instrument that focuses on micro-tonal expression!
